# Common Red Flags in Startup Ideas

## Solution looking for a problem
The founder is excited about a technology and invents a use case. Strong ideas start
from a real, observed pain, not from a cool tool.

## No willingness to pay
Users say they like it but will not pay. Free interest is not demand. If a pivot is
needed because "the value dropped," the original willingness to pay was probably weak.

## Entrenched incumbents own the workflow
If large, well-funded players already own the customer's daily workflow, a small new
entrant needs a sharp wedge (a specific underserved segment or job), not a general
"better" version.

## Top-down problem framing
The idea assumes what customers need instead of being grounded in direct customer
conversations. Bottom-up, customer-validated problems are far stronger.

## Vague target audience
"Everyone" is not a market. The tighter and more specific the initial audience, the
easier it is to reach, message, and convert.

## Verdict guidance
- pursue: painful problem, reachable audience, a real wedge, cheap to pilot.
- pivot: real problem but wrong solution/segment/business model.
- drop: weak problem, no willingness to pay, or an unwinnable market with no wedge.
