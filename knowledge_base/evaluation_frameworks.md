# Startup Idea Evaluation Frameworks

## Problem severity
A strong idea solves a painful, frequent problem. Vitamins (nice to have) lose to
painkillers (must have). Ask: how often does the problem occur, and how much does it
cost the user in money, time, or pain? Weak problems produce weak willingness to pay.

## Market potential
Estimate the market bottom-up: number of potential customers x realistic price x
adoption rate. A large but crowded market can be worse than a small, underserved one.
Beware markets that are large only in theory (everyone is a "potential" user).

## Differentiation and moat
Why you, why now, and why can't an incumbent copy this in a weekend? Real moats come
from unique data, network effects, switching costs, or a hard technical capability.
"We will execute better" is not a moat.

## Feasibility
Can a small team build a first version quickly with available resources? Ideas that
need huge capital, regulatory approval, or unproven science before any revenue are
high risk. Prefer ideas where a pilot can prove value cheaply.

## The pilot principle
The best way to de-risk a B2B idea is a small paid pilot that proves value before a
full build. If no customer will even pilot it, that is strong evidence the problem is
not painful enough.
